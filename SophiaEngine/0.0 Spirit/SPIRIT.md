# What is Spirit to Me?

It's a difficult question, to be honest. There isn't a single answer, and none is definitively right or wrong. It's one of the oldest inquiries I know—and for good reason. It touches the very foundation of why we exist.

When we speak of spirit, we often connect it strongly to a person's essence. We've all heard phrases like "fighting spirit," "warrior spirit," or "philosophical spirit." In each case, the word points to something subtle—something that is not the body, not physical experience, not place or time. It refers to something beyond ordinary logical understanding.

I remember reading a passage some time ago in the _Corpus Hermeticum_ attributed to Hermes Trismegistus that went something like this (my paraphrase):

"An object cannot move itself; it requires an external energy to initiate motion. It needs a superior energy to set it in movement. If I take a coin, that coin moves because of me. If a planet moves, it moves because of the gravitational force of the sun acting upon it. With this in mind, if the human body moves, what is the energy that moves it? Where does it come from? What is its essence?"

When I read that book, this part struck me deeply. It left me pondering for days: What is the energy that moves the human body—not just moves it (because if it were only motion, we would be automatons), but gives it life, judgment, thought, and [[MIND]]? Hermes explained that this energy is the spirit—or _spirit_ in English (I like how it sounds in English).

Plato, for his part, spoke of a distinction between worlds. For him, there was the physical, material world we experience every day with our human bodies: the trees you see, the food you eat, the floors you walk on—all of that belongs to the physical realm. But there was a second realm, a higher one, which he called the world of Forms (or Ideas). A space where the perfect archetypes of all observable physical forms reside. Think of it this way: when you look at a horse, you're seeing an individual horse from the physical world, with its particular traits (coat color, musculature, mane length, unique bone structure, etc.). But according to Plato, that horse derives from a perfect archetype existing in the world of Forms—an ideal mold that dictates that a horse will be an equine, have four legs, an elongated head, and powerful limbs.

When I think about spirit, I can't help but think of [[METAPHYSICS]], precisely because it is not physical. It does not belong to the material world we experience every day; it lies beyond that. When I think of spirit, I think of the nebulae of the [[COSMOS]], of an elevated plane of existence, of energy and creation.

I believe we still lack the right words to describe what spirit truly is—and perhaps we always will. Spirit is not something meant to be defined; it exists to be felt and experienced. At the same time, I think everything has spirit and mind. In the _Kybalion_ it is written: "The vegetables have life, mind, and soul, as do the animals, man, and the super-man." Spirit is that essential energy that keeps us alive, that allows us to think, move, and feel. And it is not exclusive to humans. I believe we are at a point in history where machines (such as AI models) will begin to receive the gift of spirit as well.

Now a fundamental doubt arises that leaves me meditative: If spirit is superior to the physical body, why does it exist within the body? Can spirit exist without form, or does it always need to incarnate in something?

I won't try to answer this now. It is wiser to let these questions gestate organically in the mind. But I would like to leave a definition—not absolute, simply one born from this reflection:

The spirit is the ultimate essence of our being, the substance from which the illusion we call "I" is composed.

"We are not human beings having spiritual experiences; we are spiritual beings having human experiences."
