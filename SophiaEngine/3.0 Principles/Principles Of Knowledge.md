# Principles Of Knowledge
---

These are foundational principles for acquiring, navigating, and deepening true knowledge—not just collecting facts, but building understanding that lasts and grows.

## Know How to Know 

Meta-learning — Learning how to learn.

Before diving deep into any subject, map the territory: How do experts organize this domain? What are the core concepts, skills, and practice methods others use? This is your "meta-map"—it prevents wandering aimlessly and accelerates progress.

Effective Search

Know where and how to look: Use precise queries, evaluate sources critically (credibility, recency, bias), cross-reference multiple perspectives, and follow trails of references. Good search is not random digging; it is targeted excavation.

Knowledge Navigation

Treat knowledge as a living network: Connect new ideas to what you already know, build hierarchies (big picture → details → applications), and create your own mental models. The best navigators don't memorize paths—they understand the landscape.



## Know How to Ask 

Specific Questions

Vague questions yield vague answers. Sharpen your query: Instead of "Tell me about X," ask "What are the key mechanisms behind X in context Y, and what evidence contradicts the mainstream view?" Precision unlocks depth.

Clear Context

Always provide (or seek) the frame: Background, assumptions, constraints, goals. A question without context is like shooting in the dark. Good questions include why it matters, what you already know, and what outcome you're after.

Critical Thinking

Every great question probes deeper: What evidence supports this? What contradicts it? What assumptions are hidden? What alternative explanations exist? Asking good questions is the engine of critical thought—it exposes flaws, reveals gaps, and sparks real insight.



## Active Practice 

Solve, Don't Copy

Passive reading or watching is weak input. Force active output: Solve problems yourself first, code it, teach it, apply it in a new context. Only then consult solutions—and compare to see where you went wrong.

Fail and Correct

Error is the best teacher when deliberate. Seek out controlled failure: Push to the edge of your ability, make mistakes on purpose if needed, then analyze exactly why. Deliberate practice thrives on feedback loops of attempt → error → insight → adjustment.

Reflect and Improve

After every session: What worked? What failed? Why? What one small change will raise the bar next time? Reflection turns experience into wisdom. Without it, repetition is just repetition—not growth.
