# Epistemology 
---

## What is epistemology?

**EPISTEMOLOGY** ἐπιστήμη (_episteme_) = knowledge λόγος (_logos_) = study, reason **The study of knowledge itself.**

It sounds strange, and even weird to stop and think about how we know that we know what we know. It seems like a tongue twister, but that's how it is. Have you ever wondered how you know what water is composed of? How do you know you're hungry? Or how do you know you're attractive? Well, that's epistemology, the questioning and study of knowledge. It's a branch of philosophy that fascinates me, precisely because it invites us to break the molds by which we interpret reality, and it forces us to search for either a truer mold or simply eliminate the molds altogether. Epistemology invites you to ask yourself why what we affirm as true is true. Why is it not false? On what basis do I affirm its veracity? When I talk about this, a very famous astrologer comes to mind, who was probably the first astrophysicist in human history. I'm referring to Kepler. He was a philosopher, astrologer, and astronomer who laid the foundations for how we understand the orbits of the planets, and one of the deepest things he postulated was the idea that there were more than 5 planets in the solar system. Not only that, he also contradicted the church, affirming that the earth and the planets were the ones that revolved around the sun, and not vice versa as was believed at that time. That said, the question arises, how did Kepler come up with this idea? Well, the first reason was because he agreed with Copernicus regarding the earth (that it revolves around the sun), and second, it was thanks to epistemology. Surely he was wondering, how do we know there are only 5 planets? How do we know that the earth is the center of the universe? Those questions led us to advances in astronomy and the understanding of the functioning of our solar system. That's why epistemology is so powerful, it invites you to question what most people take for granted. **Epistemology is powerful because it doesn't ask what to believe, but why we believe what we believe.** It reminds us that many "truths" are inherited agreements, and that the progress of knowledge begins when someone dares to ask: _What if it weren't so?_
