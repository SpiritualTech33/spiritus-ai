# Learning 
---

## **What Does It Mean to Learn?** 

Learning, at least for me, is one of the most stimulating activities I know. Sitting down to read something and walking away with new things to think about, philosophize over, and experiment with — that's nothing short of fascinating. But a question naturally arises: what does it actually mean to learn?

Most of the time, we confuse learning with memorizing. You only need to look at the average educational model to see this. They show you concepts and then test whether you memorized them. But learning isn't memorizing. I think learning has more to do with _integrating_ and _expanding_ than with storing information.

Let me give you a kitchen example. If you heat a pan on high and throw butter in, the butter will burn — it'll turn black, taste rancid, and whatever you were trying to cook will stick. Next time you make eggs with butter, you'll probably change your approach, because you learned that your previous model wasn't optimal. That's what learning is: building new mental models that align more closely with the deeper truth of how things work. You construct a model of reality with what you know, you learn something new, and you update the model. That's why reading matters so much — it gives you raw material to build newer, better, more complete models. Think of books as an architect's budget: the more you read, the bigger your budget, and the bigger the budget, the grander the structure you can build.

Now that we've defined what learning is, it would be worth exploring _how_ we learn — because there's a lot to unpack here. There isn't just one way. There are many.

---

## Learning Methods 

### **1. Through Repetition (Procedural Memory)**

This is one of the most common and intuitive forms. Repetition is, to me, one of the most powerful ways to learn. When you do something, you activate neural pathways that fire in order to accomplish that task. Cooking, for example. When you repeat the action over and over, those pathways become increasingly refined, until you reach a point where you do it automatically — you don't even have to think about it.

A clear example in my own life is baseball. I've been playing since I was seven. I probably have well over 10,000 hours of training behind me, so when I step up to bat, I don't have to think about gripping the bat with my fingers, keeping my arms close to my body, loading on time and in rhythm with the pitcher, driving my hands through the middle on the swing, keeping my weight back, and finishing with the bat up for full extension. I just _do it_ — as naturally as breathing — because I've been doing it my whole life. If you want to learn something, repeat it until it becomes part of you.

### **2. Through Insight (Sudden Reorganization)**

Let's be clear here — no mysticism needed. It's not that you suddenly learn something out of thin air. But there's a fascinating mechanism behind this, and it's deeply connected to reading and exposing yourself to different kinds of ideas. When you read — and not just read, but explore and question different concepts — you eventually start seeing patterns and algorithms in the information you receive. At some point, that accumulated information reorganizes itself in a way that leads you to an understanding you hadn't considered before, simply because you didn't have that knowledge on your hard drive yet.

This happened to me when I decided to study programming. I had been studying consciousness and its role in shaping our reality for a long time. I started noticing that our reality organizes itself based on the thoughts and beliefs we hold about ourselves. I began playing with the idea that we can "program our minds." Then one day, while meditating, the idea of studying programming hit me — suddenly, clearly. My reasoning was simple: if I can program my mind, then by studying programming, I can program my own reality. And that's how I became a programmer — one of the best decisions of my life.

### **3. Through Active Construction (Doing, Not Just Reading)**

This is a key point, and it's an open invitation to build a writing habit. Reading is great, but we learn far more when we write about what we've learned. This is because language has a unique property: it forces us to slow down our thoughts and construct them through the semantics of words. Most of us think through mental images, bodily sensations, and emotions. But when we translate all of that into words, we force our minds to reorganize that whole mass of input into language — significantly refining the cognitive process through which we handle that information. When you put it into text, you're converting mental images, emotions, and sensations into words that anyone else can read. You don't need to write entire pages or summaries. A journal or a notes app is enough. Writing matters if you want to become a better learner.

### **4. Through Connection (Associating With What You Already Know)**

This one is fairly straightforward and I talk about it in my 3C Learning Protocol. It means linking new things you learn with concepts already stored on your hard drive. For me, this works incredibly well when I'm learning about programming — connecting those concepts to philosophy or spirituality. Think of your neurons as small hard drives that store information across the totality of your brain; every small piece contains a part of the whole. When you connect new information to what's already in your brain, you allow that new knowledge to integrate with the entire system.

### **5. Through Emotion (Emotional Memory)**

This one is pretty fascinating, and it reveals a lot about the emotional nature of human beings. We tend to remember far more vividly the events that carry a strong emotional charge. I don't know of any specific mechanisms to make yourself feel emotional _before_ learning — although for me personally, it's easy, because the sheer act of learning something new already feels exciting. But a powerful exercise you can start practicing is to associate new knowledge with emotions. That way, every new thing you learn becomes a new emotional connection in your memory.

### **6. Through Teaching (The Feynman Technique)**

This is, for me, the cheat code of learning — and the best part is that it's accessible to anyone. You don't need tools, devices, or even other people. You can do it by yourself. The Feynman Principle says you learn by teaching, and it's absolutely true. When you learn a new concept, idea, or anything at all, try explaining it to someone else. If you can't explain it in simple terms, you don't truly understand it yet. But this practice of learning and then explaining is incredibly powerful — it doesn't just help you acquire new knowledge, it helps you _internalize_ it. Without a doubt, in my personal opinion, the best way to truly learn something is by teaching it.

### **7. Through Error (Learning by Getting It Wrong)**

This is an extremely powerful method of learning, but it demands a lot from you. It requires you to change the mental models you've built around mistakes and failure. Most of us tend to associate errors with failure — something negative, something to be avoided at all costs. And that's precisely why we waste a powerful opportunity to learn. There's a phrase — I can't remember where I heard it, but I believe it comes from Zen — that goes something like: "We can't control everything that happens to us in life, but we can control how we respond to it." Within the context of error, that's immensely powerful.

Imagine two perspectives: someone who is afraid of making mistakes, and someone who sees errors as teachers. Who do you think will benefit more when they stumble? The answer is obvious, isn't it?

Thanks to baseball — and more recently, programming — I've grown comfortable with mistakes, understanding them as a natural part of any learning process. Instead of seeing errors as enemies, we can observe them as teachers. When I get struck out, instead of getting frustrated and feeling guilty for not helping my team, I can approach it from a place of peace and ask: _what did this at-bat teach me?_ When I get the logic wrong in a program and have to debug, I can ask: _what did I fail to account for that caused this error?_ If you look at it from this angle, every mistake is a small opportunity to learn. Don't waste them with negativity — transmute them into something positive and learn from them. Errors exist to show us where we still need to keep working.

---

There are other ways, but I think these are among the most important. Learning is an invaluable skill today, because as Yuval Noah Harari would say, the greatest asset of our time is knowledge. It's important not only to understand what learning is, but also to know the mechanisms that optimize it.

_Learning isn't knowing more — it's seeing differently._ _And whoever learns how to learn never stops growing._
