# Intuition
---

## What is the intuition?

Well, as is my custom, I'll start by asking questions—because questioning is the most fitting way to open yourself to knowledge. Questioning invariably leads to two things:

1. An open acceptance that we don't know what we're asking about.
2. The subsequent search for answers to truly understand the concept in question.

With that said, I'll ask myself: What is intuition? Why is it so powerful? How is it built? What are the foundations of a solid intuition? And I'll meditate on these questions in the lines that follow.

First things first. Let's talk about a definition for intuition. I'm not interested in absolute definitions; I just want to verbalize this phenomenon.

I recently read a quote from Magnus Carlsen, one of the best chess players today—if not the best. He said something like: "Many times I can't explain a particular move; I just know it's the right one, and it seems my intuition is right more often than it might appear."

Leaving aside the fact that he explicitly uses the word "intuition," I want us to analyze a pattern that shows up very often in the context of intuition. When you reach a certain level of mastery in any field (be it a sport, art, literature, music, any physical or mental skill), something very curious happens: we begin to abstract much of the information needed to perform that action.

Let me give two examples I'm intimately familiar with. When I learn a new programming concept, I tend to abstract a lot from previously acquired knowledge. For instance, if I'm studying parallelism, my mental models for interpreting that information will already have internalized variables, data types, loops, conditionals, functions, programming paradigms, databases, etc. I'm not consciously thinking about them, but they're operating in the background to help me grasp the new concept.

When I play baseball—whether training or in a game—and I step up to bat, I'm not consciously thinking about placing my feet shoulder-width apart, maintaining an athletic stance, lining up my knuckles, keeping my weight back, fixing my eyes on the contact point with the ball, driving my hands through the middle of the field after contact for extension, or reading the spin of the seams to identify the pitch type. I don't think about any of that. My brain simply abstracts the entire task and encapsulates it into an internalized concept I call "hitting." For me, it's as natural as breathing because I've been doing it my whole life—it's second nature.

When you've been doing something for long enough, it's natural to develop a certain kind of intuition—a sixth sense that whispers valuable information in key moments. For example, if I'm in an unfavorable count—say 2 strikes and 1 ball—my intuition will read the pitcher, draw from my experience, and tell me something like: "It's very likely he'll come with a waste pitch; he won't throw it near the zone." What my brain is doing is pulling thousands of chunks of information from similar situations and observing what happened in those cases. From that data, I get a prediction.

It sounds like a very mathematical process—and in part, it is—but human intuition has a more subtle, more energetic quality that can't be fully explained by neurological or purely mathematical processes alone. When I step up to bat and see the pitcher's facial expression and body language, the energy he transmits to me and I transmit back, my history with that pitcher, the game situation, the runners on base, the pitches he has in his arsenal—all those variables lead to a conclusion I can't fully explain, but something inside me says it's right.

I feel this even more strongly when I'm running the bases. There's an inner voice that tells me when to steal. I intuitively sense when the pitcher is going to pick off first and when he won't. Deep down, I know—before he even makes the throw—that he's not going to turn to first, and that's when I go. Honestly, I don't know how I know it, but in the moment, I have zero doubt.

## Shu-Ha-RI

Let's use a Japanese concept that illustrates this beautifully: **SHU-HA-RI**.

These are the three stages of mastery in martial arts, which curiously apply to mastery in anything:

- **SHU** — The learning phase. No experimentation, no intuition yet—just imitation of the master, learning from him, understanding the principles. It's the guided phase.
- **HA** — Here you start breaking the mold, experimenting, testing. Which methods work best for me? Which do I like and which don't? It's the phase of self-knowledge. You begin to understand the _why_ behind things.
- **RI** — This is the final stage. The rules disappear—not because they no longer exist, but because they've become part of your being. They're absorbed into your neural processes. You no longer think about performing the action—you _are_ the action. In my case, I no longer think about writing code—I _am_ the code. I no longer think about hitting—I _am_ the hitting. I no longer think about meditating—I _am_ the meditation. It's at this point that intuition truly blossoms.

I think intuition is an extraordinarily powerful art when used responsibly. There are other kinds of intuition too—for example, the energetic kind. I'm very sensitive to people's electromagnetic fields, and intuitively I can tell which people carry positive energy and which don't. Things as simple as micro-expressions, word choice, or the topics they choose to talk about give me a lot of information about what kind of consciousness they have.

There's an almost mystical, metaphysical dimension to intuition. I believe intuition isn't just pattern-matching similar data to predict an event. I feel that the deepest intuition—the kind I'm experiencing and talking about now—is a connection to something larger than ourselves. It's as if your consciousness is linked to some higher intelligence that has the ability to whisper information to you. Our task is to interpret those whispers.

---

## Conclussion

That said, I also have to clarify that intuition is extremely subtle. It's not a loud noise that takes over your entire cognitive load—it's a gentle whisper that only appears in the silence of the mind. When your mind is cluttered with noise, worries, anxiety, negative desires, and emotions, it's difficult—almost impossible—to hear the voice of intuition. There must be mental clarity for intuition to speak without restriction. Eat well, train, meditate, study—and you'll notice how your intuition begins to flourish.

To conclude, I could say that intuition is a skill of the SOUL, lent to the physical body to guide us along the right path. It is SOPHIA and NOUS speaking to us directly.
