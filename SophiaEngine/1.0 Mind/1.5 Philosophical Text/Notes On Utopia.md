# Notes On Utopia
---

## Intro

I recently concluded my reading of one of Thomas More’s books, titled Utopia. I want to take advantage of the time I have while waiting in line for public transportation to write my impressions of the book and the society that More proposes in this work.

---

## Meaning Of Word Utopia

Let’s start with the basics, which is the meaning of the word Utopia; in modern times, it is used to refer to an ideal institutional model, where everything works wonderfully. But its Greek origin refers to “a place that does not exist,” and in my personal opinion, I prefer the second term, since such a place does not yet exist in the world.

---

## Society's Without Money

That said, I want to talk about the central axis that drives More’s text, which is the functioning of a society without money. Although this may seem impossible, More shows how a society could function without the use of money, delegating responsibilities and jobs equally and indistinctly between the sexes. They take turns in one-year shifts for agriculture and food collection for the country. There are public dispensaries, in which there is food in abundance for all. This philosophy is also nourished from the perspective of eating only what is sufficient, no more, no less.

He also alludes to scholars, who, due to their particular affinity for study, may not do manual labor and can direct their lives to the study of philosophy and sciences existing in Utopia.

---

## Religion and Spirituality

I would also like to highlight the religion and spirituality practiced in Utopia. Since everyone in the nation agrees that there is a creator god, a supreme god that rises above everything, but they also give freedom for each individual to profess their faith and love for other spirits and myths. This point seems extremely relevant to me, since many of the problems of nations come from which religion has the moral and ideological control of the population. By giving freedom to express your faith, with respect and love, you allow for healthy coexistence. They emphasize that no one should mention the names of their personal spirits or gods; the only God that is mentioned is the supreme creator.

There are no hierarchies or positions, except for religious positions, such as priests and the leaders of each family, who are chosen by common agreement.

There is also a strong connection with the senate and much respect for its laws and norms.

Indeed, the model of society that More describes is utopian, idealistic, I would say. But I think that such societies can be aspired to in the future.

----

## Precious Stones and Jewelry

I would also like to touch on the way in which Utopians relate to precious stones and jewelry. They consider it low; they use gold and silver to mark criminals and people who do not respect the norms. The chains of prisoners are made of gold, and they are filled with silver and gold to be easily identified. This generates a new paradigm regarding the way in which Western cultures associate with jewelry. Most people think that precious stones show power, status, and occupation. This seems interesting to me since putting jewelry as an exhibition of your capital power is only a sample of how materialistic Western ideology is, and how we give more importance to material abundance than to spiritual abundance.

---

## Utopians Philosophy

They also talk about the Utopians’ philosophy of life. He mentions the delights of life, which I interpreted as pleasures, making it clear that for them, the maximum delight is that of good health, explaining that when health is lacking, nothing can be enjoyed.

They live from a philosophy of zero material attachment, finding pleasure in spirituality, religious practices, and in being useful to their nation.

---

## Conclusion

In conclusion, More proposes a system of nation that may sound impossible, since it aims to turn nations into large families, rather than masses of people.

But I think that if institutional changes begin to be made and we start to approach human education based on values such as love, compassion, and gratitude, and we create systems that feed the idea of detachment from goods (that is, that our value as an individual does not come from our material accumulation, but from our participation with the nation and with our brothers), we could begin to take the first steps towards the establishment of a utopian republic.

To be honest, I believe that More left the blueprint to create a prosperous, egalitarian nation that opposes contemporary capitalist ideals. It may be impossible to recreate a society identical to the one described in the text, but ideas can be taken from there and used to create a more prosperous, egalitarian, and fair model of republic.

I clarify that this is a summary; I only took the ideas that resonated most with me. The details are in the book.​​​​​​​​​​​​​​​​
