# The Illusion Of Language
---

It would be fitting to say that language is also a form of illusion. But before beginning the argument for such a claim, it would be necessary to first analyze how language stands as the primary form of knowledge and what allows us to eliminate abstraction within the ideas we develop.

When you wish to learn something, to understand it deeply, you need the ability to verbalize that thought or concept. Think of it this way: when you want to share your ideas with another person, you must verbalize those ideas, and through language, you allow your ideas to be received by another consciousness. Well, many people conclude that language is the basis of people’s thinking, when in reality we think more with images, sensations, or sounds.

But when we shift our observation of the mind and focus on words, on language, we slow down the speed at which we think, allowing us to analyze in detail each of the concepts that cross the mind. This produces a deepening of the content of whatever is there (referring to the mind).

Now, when we teach a concept that we master, using words as the medium, it is in that moment that we truly learn, because as I said before, we have to take that concept piece by piece and build it into words that have meaning and coherence. That’s why I affirm that when you teach something, you learn that something.

To give an example, I program; if I think of a function in Python, I remember the syntax, I have a mental image of myself sitting at my laptop, with VS Code open, and the form of the function. That’s a mental image. If I had to explain what a function in Python is, I would have to take that mental image and break it down into words. That process would solidify the knowledge I have, teaching me what I think I already know.

With that said, I conclude that a beneficial practice for better understanding whatever you’re studying or learning is to explain it to yourself. Though it may sound strange, I don’t fear being wrong when I say that it’s very healthy and a sign of good intelligence to talk to oneself. Internal dialogue is one of the most powerful forms of learning.

Now, if language has so much power, how can it be that at the same time it’s a mere illusion? Think of it this way: who invented the language you speak? Why are certain types of sounds used to express certain types of ideas? Why do we call it sun and tree? Why do meanings mean what they mean?

I read about a people—honestly, I don’t remember the name—but they explained that in that community there were 50 different words to refer to snow. Thick snow, light snow, melting snow, falling snow. One word for different versions of the same concept. That simple fact makes them conceptualize snow in a way we can’t even imagine. Words grant meaning to the world, but that world is not an inherent one. It’s a world constructed by ourselves, since we are the ones who decide to assign those meanings to those sounds.

That’s why I conclude that language is also a mere illusion.
