# Transcendence Of Language
---

Interesting. This Wittgenstein is a deep thinker.

When I started programming, I first began by asking myself what language is. This is how most courses begin, talking about the differences between natural and formal language.

Then, over time and observing Unicode characteristics, it made me rethink my own precepts regarding language. It made me realize that each language is nothing more than a grouping of symbols in a specific order that generate a contingent meaning. But that meaning does not exist by itself; it exists intersubjectively in the collective imagination. It is the conventions and linguistic agreements that give meaning to language. Now, if language is the piece by which we give meaning to the world, we name it, and by naming it, we make it understandable, it is necessary to affirm that our interpretations of the world are not ours; they are shared by the shared meaning we give to each word.

I think my perspective is more aligned with the second Wittgenstein.

Now I think that language becomes a filter through which we interpret reality. In fact, it is curious because you can study the same concept in different languages, English and Spanish for example. And although it is the same concept, the way of assimilating the concept becomes different because the filter through which the concept is experienced is different.

It's incredible how you perceive the world once you become aware of this.

You no longer associate meaning and argument. You understand that what exists, exists independently of whether or not there is a word to name it. What would happen if the word existence did not exist? Existence would continue to exist, but we could not name it, and therefore we would not know about it. That also leaves me thinking about all the things that are not yet named but that exist without us knowing it.

What does it really mean to exist?
