# Language
---

## What is the language?

I believe we spend far too much time in our lives talking, assigning meanings to our experiences through language, without ever pausing to consider where it comes from, why it is the way it is, or why it exists in the first place.

If I had to define language, I would say it is the grouping of symbols for the creation of meanings contingent upon our subjective experiences—what philosophers call "Qualia." Language, more than being something absolute, is nothing but an instrument that allows us to externalize internal experiences through words.

---

## Where does the language come from?

I think language originates and is born from the intrinsic human need for connection. That desire to validate that what I feel, what I experience, also lives in the consciousness of others. Not just for that reason—language's birth arises from various perspectives, like survival, for example. Yuval Noah Harari argues that it was thanks to language that we could organize ourselves into civilizations: mentioning where there was food, where predators abounded, and how to organize ourselves to enhance the effectiveness of each individual in society. From an evolutionary perspective, language stands as the foundational stone upon which human civilization was built.

---

## Language As An Illusion

That said, I pause to meditate for a moment on its illusory nature, because indeed, language is an illusion. Not because it isn't real—it is; language has built nations, science, philosophy, literature, cinema, and an endless array of things that wouldn't have existed without it. The illusory nature of language stems from the fact that, in itself, it is nothing more than a construct of intersubjective conventions that generate meaning from the grouping of those symbols. Why does "apple" mean what it means? Why does that specific grouping of Latin symbols have that concrete meaning? At what point did we begin assigning sense to symbols created by ourselves? It's recursive if you think about it that way.

---

## Exist Meaning Without Language?

On the last question—and the one I want to expand on the most, since I connect it deeply with Zen—is whether there exists an intrinsic meaning prior to language. Zen seeks precisely that state: the profound internal silence where you cease to be a slave to language and live in that experience prior to it. Does something like pre-language existence exist?

---

## Conclusion

I can conclude that yes, it does. When you wake up in the morning and feel the dew on the grass beneath your feet. Or when you meditate and feel that peace flooding your body; when you eat something you've craved intensely and feel pleasure in doing so—all of that is loaded with meaning. The experience itself fills your human existence with significance. Put another way, there is something prior to language, prior even to thought itself. What is that which exists before everything? Before you even begin to think? Descartes said, "I think, therefore I am," and in a certain sense, it's true—you only exist for yourself when you begin to think. But what is there before thought? Before even existence?

I don't pretend to arrive at all the answers; I simply hope to keep asking, because in asking, I question, and in questioning, I exist.
