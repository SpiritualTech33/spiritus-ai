# Science
---

## What is Science?

Science, a profoundly powerful word, one that surely occupies some space in the [[MIND]] of every human being. I write this note because my goal is to become an engineer-scientist-philosopher. And to achieve that, I need to understand science from its origins to its very essence.

### 1. ETYMOLOGICAL ROOT: The Act of Distinguishing

### From Latin _Scientia_

**Scientia** ← **Scire** (to know, to understand)

But here's where it gets deep:

**Scire** is related to the Proto-Indo-European root ***skei-**, which means:

- **To cut**
- **To separate**
- **To divide**
- **To distinguish**

Science is a way of thinking that allows us to discriminate knowledge. To subject it to experimentation and, through it, determine whether the assumptions we formulate are true or not. It's epistemological cleanliness and hygiene.

Imagine, as Richard Feynman might say, the sheer delight in slicing through the confusion, peeling back layers to reveal what's really there—not with a knife, but with questions and tests. And Carl Sagan would add that this act of distinguishing echoes across the cosmos, separating the myths from the majestic realities of stars and atoms.

### 2. OPERATIONAL DEFINITION

**Science is:** A methodological system for producing knowledge that seeks to describe, explain, and predict natural phenomena through the construction of falsifiable models based on empirical observation, controlled experimentation, and logical-mathematical reasoning.

Let's unpack this definition, because it's dense—packed tight like the core of a neutron star.

It's a methodological system because knowledge doesn't spring up spontaneously; it has operational rules, grounded in the [[SCIENTIFIC METHOD]]. We use this method to discern whether something can be considered "scientifically verifiable" or not. It's reproducible, meaning others can verify those assumptions, and it's systemic, building upon itself to generate new and more complex knowledge. It transforms ignorance into comprehension and creates new models for interpreting reality.

It describes, predicts, and explains observable phenomena. For example, describing the gravitational force exerted by the sun and how planets are affected by it. Then, through observation and analysis, explaining how this occurs, and finally, using the generated information to predict the orbits of the planets over the next 100 years.

Science is built on falsifiable models, meaning something is considered scientific only if it can be refuted through experience. For instance, we might claim that all Homo sapiens can see, but there are people incapable of doing so, so we can affirm that not all of us have the capacity for vision.

### **Empirical Observation**

**Empiria** (ἐμπειρία) = experience

Science anchors itself in the observable, measurable, quantifiable.

**But beware:** "Observable" includes things like:

- Cosmic microwave background radiation
- Subatomic particles (tracks in detectors)
- Gravitational waves (laser interferometry)

Not just "what you see with your eyes." As Feynman loved to point out, the universe is full of wonders we can't see directly, but we can tease them out with clever instruments and a bit of ingenuity.

### **Logical-Mathematical Reasoning**

Science uses two fundamental tools:

**Deductive logic:** From the general to the particular

- If all metals conduct electricity (premise)
- And copper is a metal (premise)
- Then copper conducts electricity (necessary conclusion)

[[MATHEMATICS]] The language of quantitative precision

- Without mathematics: "Gravity attracts objects"
- With mathematics: F = G(m₁m₂)/r²

Equations capture **invariant relationships**. They are the deep structure upholding phenomena.

**Galileo:** "The universe is written in mathematical language."

And oh, the joy in that, as Feynman would exclaim—the thrill of seeing patterns emerge, like discovering a hidden puzzle in nature's playground.

## Why Does It Exist?

Science emerges for a multitude of reasons; it even seems inevitable, this branch of thought appearing like a star igniting in the void. Let's think first about its earliest steps. In its beginnings, it was rudimentary: observing patterns in animals, determining which plants were edible and which weren't, where it was safe to sleep, which areas to avoid due to predators. It all started as a survival necessity. We had to observe our surroundings and draw conclusions to survive. Think of it this way: the groups that were better observers and reached better conclusions about their environment had higher chances of survival. Science, at first, exists because of our innate survival instinct, combined with the human brain's capacity to think and reason more deeply than the rest of the biosphere's animals.

Another strong reason for science's existence is the particular neurological condition of the human brain. There's a concept called cognitive neoteny, referring to the fact that the human brain remains curious and playful even into adulthood. I'll use cats as a reference point here, because I love them and have several at home, so I'm very familiar with their behavior. When cats are young, they're incredibly curious, active, and playful. They climb practically any structure within reach, play with almost every animal they see, and practice hunting methods with their companions. As they grow, they become more grumpy, stop playing and exploring the world. They hunt to eat, clean themselves, and sleep, losing that childish charm. With humans, something curious happens: regardless of age, we remain curious about the world and its complexities. Our brains compulsively seek patterns; in fact, there are specialized circuits for pattern-seeking. Every time we discover a pattern, our brain releases dopamine, and that's why science is so rewarding for those who practice it. With each new discovery, each new understanding, there's a biochemical reward waiting. So, seen that way, science arises as a response to our particular neurological conditions.

Science also exists due to the inefficiency of ancient mental models we used to interpret the world. Before science, we relied on common sense, intuition, tradition, authority, revelation, and philosophical speculation to make sense of things. But if we analyze each one closely, we see why they were deficient. Common sense often backfires because, in general, things aren't as obvious as they seem. People once believed the Earth was flat because it looked flat, and by common sense, it had to be. Today, we know that's not so.

Intuition, though powerful in some cases, is limited because it's heavily influenced by personal biases.

Tradition and authority have never been the best ways to consolidate knowledge, because they relegate all questioning of phenomena to a single person or a small group, without the possibility of challenging those assumptions since they come from authority. It's the classic "We do it this way because it's always been done that way." Look at what happened with the church and those who dared question it before the Renaissance. They were accused of heresy or witchcraft. Giordano Bruno was burned in 1600 for proposing the universe was infinite. Galileo was forced to recant under threat of torture. Authority without verification is epistemic tyranny.

There's revelation, and here I want to pause, because many great scientific advances have come from revelations. Descartes said the bases of his method were revealed through dreams. Kekulé discovered the structure of benzene in a dream of a snake biting its tail. Tesla claimed to receive blueprints of his machines directly in his mind. Socrates spoke of a Daemon. Einstein discovered relativity by imagining riding a beam of light. Revelations have been many; the only problem is they're not replicable—a gift granted to a few extraordinary minds throughout history.

And finally, philosophical speculation. I think this is the starting point for generating any scientific assumption; after all, hypothesis formulation comes precisely from speculating on a topic. But we can't rely solely on speculation for knowledge. And that's why science was born: because we needed a way of thinking that allowed us to create true, replicable, verifiable knowledge.

There's an even deeper and more powerful cause: the universe invites being known. Science exists because the [[COSMOS]] allows it to. There are unchanging constants, like the speed of light or our planet's gravitational force. At its core, the universe has a coherent mathematical structure that can be studied. If the universe were chaotic, unpredictable, without laws, science couldn't exist. But the universe is ordered, harmonious, coherent. It's thanks to this fact that science can exist as we know it. And it's not just me saying this; great geniuses from different eras have noticed the same.

**Galileo:** "The nature is written in mathematical language."

**Eugene Wigner (physicist, Nobel):** "The unreasonable effectiveness of mathematics in the natural sciences is something bordering on the mysterious."

**Why do equations invented by humans describe galaxies millions of light-years away?**

**Kepler:**

> "Geometry existed before Creation. It is co-eternal with the mind of God... Geometry provided God with a model for Creation."

**Einstein (agnostic, not theist):**

> "The most incomprehensible thing about the universe is that it is comprehensible."

**Newton:** Saw his laws as divine thoughts encoded in mathematics.

I don't pretend to give a final answer on why science exists. I think some questions invite questioning but never closure. If I had to define something, I'd say science exists because the universe invites exploration, as if it wants us to understand it and has granted us the gift of science to comprehend it.

Like Sagan gazing at the stars, we feel that pull—the cosmos whispering its secrets, urging us onward. And Feynman would chuckle, saying it's all about the pleasure of finding things out, no more, no less.

## The Genesis of Science: Where Does It Begin?

We've explored what science is and why it exists. Now it's time to ask when it began. At what point in human history could we establish that science started?

Well, defining an exact point when science began is an extremely difficult exercise—and frankly, futile. Science doesn't have a single cradle; it was born multiple times and reinvented itself into what we know today. To uncover these cradles, we must take a short stroll through humanity's long history, going back about 2.5 million years to observe our ancestors. It all starts with proto-science—not formal science, but already showing glimpses of sequential and observational thinking. One of the first tools used by humans were sharpened stones as weapons. The premise was simple: observe a sharp stone, perhaps an accident or mere instinctive curiosity led Homo habilis to strike it, suddenly noticing it became sharper. Over time, they developed techniques for sharper stones to defend themselves. We couldn't say science begins here, but several parts align with the scientific process:

- Observation (this stone is sharp)
- Hypothesis (if I hit it, it'll be sharper)
- Experimentation (hit the stone)
- Verification (works/doesn't work)
- Refinement (improved technique)

Time marched on, and Homo sapiens developed (that's us), bringing what might be the most important revolution in our species' history: the cognitive revolution. The most accepted theory comes from the cooking hypothesis. It explains that discovering fire, and thus cooking food, reduced the energy needed to digest meals. That surplus energy went to the brain, following the law of conservation of energy (energy is neither created nor destroyed, only transformed). Whatever the cause, there's a marked fact: the development of complex language, abstract thought, and the ability to think and speak about things that don't exist or we can't see. This is evidenced by cave paintings, musical instruments, and myths of ancient tribes worshiping forest spirits, rivers, or mountains. Science as we know it hadn't been born yet, but surely hunter-gatherer bands looked at the sky every night and wondered what those beautiful shining lights were. In fact, after the agricultural revolution, sky observation became vital, as stars marked seasons for planting and harvesting, serving as guides to avoid getting lost in the world's vastness. Here, we start seeing deep glimpses of scientific thinking's development.

From this initial stage of science, we quantum leap to two great civilizations that left their legacy through contributions to transmitting human knowledge across generations: the Sumerians and Egyptians.

Writing, as far as we know today, was invented by the Sumerians with their cuneiform system. They wrote on stone tablets. They invented the sexagesimal system we use so automatically—if you've ever wondered why hours have 60 minutes, why minutes 60 have seconds, and circles 360 degrees, thank the Sumerians. They developed mathematics, primitive algebra, astronomy, the zodiac, among other things. Now we're seeing science. I'm not sure I'd dare say Mesopotamia and the Sumerians are the parents of science, but they are the parents of writing, and that's saying a lot. Thanks to them, we can look back and learn what our ancestors thought.

We also have Egypt, who took the first steps in medicine, geometry, and mathematics too. The Pyramids of Giza are irrefutable proof; they used the Pythagorean theorem 1,000 years before it was established.

Continuing through the fascinating history of human thought, we arrive at a place renowned as one of the most influential cradles in Western thinking: Greece. Seedbed of minds that walked into posterity for their ideas and ability to see beyond the era's paradigms. True revolutionaries. The list is robust, but worth mentioning.

We have Thales of Miletus, who can be considered the first Western philosopher-scientist. Before Thales, most, if not all, explanations about the world's functioning came from myth. If someone asked why it rained, the answer was often that Zeus was angry. Thales disagreed with the era's explanations, so he developed his own, not invoking gods, seeking natural causes. Though not named then, he was advocating for naturalism, materialism, and rationalism. He used geometry to measure pyramids, predicted eclipses, and proposed everything was water (one of the first theories to unify matter).

We have Pythagoras, whom you've surely heard of in high school with his famous theorem: a² + b² = c². He was one of the first to say the universe was mathematical; more precisely, that numbers govern the universe.

We have Democritus, who posited that really, the only things that exist are atoms and the void. It's not just surprising—it's counterintuitive to think someone without microscopes or experiments, through pure reason, imagination, and intuition, 2,300 years before Dalton, was already talking about atoms in Greece.

And to close, we have [[Aristoteles]], father of biology for categorizing over 500 animal species, father of physics, meteorology, and psychology. One of the greatest thinkers in all Western philosophy. Here, we took a gigantic leap, but the verifiable part was still missing.

Continuing to the point in human history that [[CARL SAGAN]] considers humanity's golden age, we have the legendary city of Alexandria and its colossal library. With over 700,000 papyri and parchments encompassing knowledge from Greek, Mesopotamian, Egyptian, Indian, and Persian civilizations. Back then, it stored that part of the world's knowledge.

Among the most prominent figures in that city, we have Euclid, Archimedes, Eratosthenes, and Dionysius. Four titans of human thought. Think of it this way: there's a branch of geometry named Euclidean geometry, and it's on that base that many interpretations of the universe's shape were born, talking about planes and three-dimensionality. Euclid was the father of mathematics for over 2,000 years. Archimedes' lever principle, the famous phrase "Give me a fulcrum and I will move the world," developed an experimental scientific method, and is one of the first documented uses of mathematics to describe physics. We have Eratosthenes, who without satellites, internet, or even telephone or radio communication, measured Earth's circumference. Using mathematics, ingenuity, and creativity. The story is even fun: he measured shadows of wooden sticks buried in the ground at different points on the planet at the same time, through triangulation between the distance between those sticks and their shadows' angles, calculated Earth's circumference. And finally Dionysius, the [[Ludwig Wittgenstein]] before Christ. He was one of the first, if not the first, to delimit language's limits and study its structure. As I said, great titans of human thought.

This period could be defined as the establishment of superior thought. We began creating truly abstract ideas, deciphering our mind's structure, but much was still missing because there was no way to verify those assumptions.

From here, we jump to the Renaissance. Europe went through a dark age during the Middle Ages. Religion and the church were the intellectual leaders and truth-bearers then. While Europe remained in shadows, Islamic scholars preserved and expanded science. Al-Khwarizmi founded algebra and algorithms, and Ibn al-Haytham is one of the fathers of the modern scientific method.

**His method:**

1. Observation of the problem
2. **Hypothesis formulation**
3. **Experimentation to verify**
4. Conclusion

This is the modern scientific method, 400 years before Descartes or Bacon.

But back to Europe, after the dark times of the Middle Ages, we arrive at the Renaissance, an era that saw thinkers who are institutional today.

We have Copernicus, who proposed the heliocentric theory, refuting the accepted geocentric theory. Galileo, who used the first telescope for astronomical purposes, first observing Jupiter's moons, Venus's phases, and sunspots.

**Galileo said:** "Philosophy is written in this grand book continually open before our eyes (I speak of the universe), but it cannot be understood unless one first learns to comprehend the language and know the characters in which it is written. It is written in mathematical language."

Well, we've toured human history, analyzed contributions from the most extraordinary minds to walk the Earth, and all this beautiful process leads to the crystallization of modern science. The birth of science as we understand it today: the Scientific Revolution. In this process, there are many names, but I think three institutionalized science.

First, René Descartes, French philosopher who developed methodical doubt. He held that we can only know if something is true if we first doubt that assumption. We must deduce from first principles. We have Francis Bacon, who developed the inductive method:

- Observation
- Data collection
- Induction of general principles
- **Experimentation to verify**

And Isaac Newton, who developed the supreme synthesis in Principia: three laws of motion and the law of universal gravitation. For the first time, the same model explained phenomena on Earth and in the [[COSMOS]]—in other words, the unification of terrestrial and celestial physics.

|**Place/Era**|**Contribution**|
|---|---|
|**Africa (~2.5M years)**|Causal thinking (tools)|
|**Mesopotamia (3500 BCE)**|Systematic astronomy, mathematics|
|**Egypt (3000 BCE)**|Empirical medicine, geometry|
|**Greece (600 BCE)**|Naturalism, rationalism, logic|
|**Alexandria (300 BCE)**|Synthesis, geometric method|
|**Islam (800 CE)**|Experimental method, preservation|
|**Europe (1500 CE)**|Scientific revolution, mathematization|

Returning to the initial question about science's birth. It would be impossible to establish an exact point; in fact, I think there isn't one. While modern science emerges from the Scientific Revolution, the truth is science has been brewing since humans could reason. It's not immutable; it's a continuous process building on existing foundations. Science is the union of human knowledge, refuted, tested, and analyzed from different perspectives. Science doesn't born; it's made, and in that making, we approach truth.

Science doesn't begin in a place or date, but at the intersection of a regular world, a vulnerable body, a curious mind, and a community capable of self-correction.

As Sagan might muse, it's woven into the fabric of our star-stuff heritage, and Feynman would grin, saying it's just us humans poking at the universe to see what happens.

## Philosophy of Science

Let's set aside the technicalities, treatises, contributions, names. Eliminate all that. Not because it's noise, but because I want to analyze science from its root, from its purest philosophical perspective. We've explored what science is, why it exists, where we suppose it begins, and we leave its philosophy for last because it's at the bottom of everything—the soul of science itself, its operating system, its source code.

Science, in my view, is humanity's noblest and most honest endeavor. It doesn't take its assumptions and sell them as unquestionable truths; instead, it does something humbler: It says, we don't know everything, and what we think we know might also be wrong. Still, we won't tire of asking, questioning, and testing, because that's the only way to approach truth.

Sagan said science is a candle in a dark room. It illuminates a little, letting us see nearby, but we're still blind to the full panorama.

Science is a process, not a product. Think of it this way: there's a scientific paradigm. That paradigm explains reality or its phenomena. Over time, new evidence refutes that paradigm, and a new one is created, and so on.

**Thomas Kuhn** showed that science advances by:

1. **Normal science**: Work within a paradigm
2. **Crisis**: Anomalies the paradigm can't explain
3. **Revolution**: Paradigm shift
4. **New paradigm**: New normal science established

That's the beauty of science: no definitive answers, no permanent theories—everything is contingent and subject to change. And that essence, the lack of dogmatism and commitment to truth, makes science so delicious and appealing to minds seeking truth. Christiaan Huygens said, "The world is my country and science my religion," and what a beautiful statement. I'd polish it by saying the universe is my country, and science the language that lets me explain it, at least partially. Because science knows less than it doesn't, and yet we keep exploring and investigating.

The philosophy of science at its core is asking about what we don't know, because in every well-formulated question, we get closer to truth—and truth is the ultimate good.

Nothing left but to thank [[GOD]], [[SOPHIA]] the [[NOUS]] and the [[COSMOS]] for allowing me to practice the noble art of science. May they accompany us in this continuous discovery of the world and ourselves.

**Karl Popper:** "Science never pursues the illusory aim of making its answers final. It advances toward an infinite but unattainable goal: that of ever discovering new, deeper, and more general problems."

## Does Science Describe the World… or Describe Us Trying to Understand It?

This question isn't made to be answered, but meditated upon.

When we talk about describing the world, there's no perennial world out there; the world is as we perceive it and interpret it, but we're never exposed to the world as it is.

I think the answer is both: science describes the world as we're capable of perceiving it, and describes how we perceive the world. It's a symbiotic relationship—without an observer, who defines the world? And without a world, what can we define? One doesn't exist without the other.

I don't pretend to give a final answer to this question, just let my mind use it to keep exploring the infinity of knowledge, and keep wondering: how much is left for us to know?
