# Electromagnetism spectrum - The limits of perception
---

Just a few hours ago, I read something that blew my mind. It was in Carl Sagan's book _Cosmos_, specifically in Chapter 4. The concept is called the **electromagnetic spectrum**, and it's nothing short of fascinating. It revealed something I've been intuiting for a while now: the reality we experience is merely a tiny fraction of the total reality.

Let's start with the question—because doubt is what leads us to answers. What is the electromagnetic spectrum? A simplistic definition would be: all electromagnetic radiation that exists, organized by wavelength (or frequency).

## Key Components

**1. Electromagnetic Radiation**

- Energy traveling in the form of waves
- Doesn't require a physical medium (travels through vacuum)
- Includes: light, heat, radio waves, X-rays, etc.

**2. Wavelength**

- Distance between two peaks of the wave
- Measured in meters (m), nanometers (nm), etc.
- **Longer** = lower energy (radio waves)
- **Shorter** = higher energy (gamma rays)

**3. Frequency**

- Number of waves passing a point per second
- Measured in Hertz (Hz)
- Frequency and wavelength are **inversely proportional**
- Long wavelength = low frequency
- Short wavelength = high frequency

They all travel at a constant speed: the speed of light. Because technically, they _are_ light.

## How much light do we see?

Now, here's where it gets fun—and deeply unsettling. Things really start to twist when you bring [[PERCEPTION]] into the equation.

The human eye can only detect a range from about 400 to 700 nanometers in wavelength. That means every color, every texture, every shape we can appreciate exists within that narrow band. We have three primary colors—red, blue, and green—and all the hues we perceive are combinations of these, creating the rich palette of our world.

But here's the kicker: if I told you that all those colors you can see make up less than 0.0035% of the total light in the universe? It sounds absurd, but let's illustrate it.

What does this really mean? It's curious because it echoes [[Kant]] and his "thing-in-itself." Most of us take for granted that reality is exactly as we perceive it: the sky is blue, the earth is brown, blood is red. Bees are yellow, purple represents royalty and spirituality. But the spectrum shows us there is no such thing as "red" or "yellow" out there in an absolute sense—those are interpretations our eyes and brains provide for the wavelengths we can detect. Colors are human interpretations of the visible slice of the spectrum. The rest? We simply can't see it.

Kant argued that our reality is filtered—we don't experience the world as it truly is, but through the sieve of our physical senses. The "thing-in-itself," raw reality, lies beyond our perception.

Try a simple exercise: imagine a completely new color. Not a mix of existing ones—a brand-new one. Hard, right? That's because our interpretations are bounded by our phenomenological experience. We can only know what we can think, name, and feel—and to do any of those, we need senses to conceptualize it. Colors don't "exist" in reality; they're your brain's translation of specific wavelengths of light.

## Conclussion 

Seeing this sparks a natural thought: what colors, what forms, what beauty must the [[COSMOS]] hold across the full spectrum? Imagine, just for a moment, perceiving the electromagnetic waves from radio antennas, your WiFi signal, the heat fields around living beings, the auras of people. Imagine seeing the spectra of planets, constellations, galaxies, comets. Seeing what is invisible yet felt. Imagine colors beyond the observable range. Even trying to picture it strains the limits of my imagination and reshapes how I view the world.

Different animal species can see wider or narrower ranges than we can. Bees, for example, see ultraviolet light, revealing landing patterns on flowers like neon maps. Snakes detect infrared, seeing the heat emitted by living creatures. For them, reality is fundamentally different. Perhaps in their vision, "red" doesn't exist the way it does for us.

It's a fascinating, almost abstract topic. Once again, it reminds me how small we are, and how much remains to explore in this marvelous journey of wonder and discovery we call science.

As Sagan might say, staring at the vastness, we are like children playing on the shore while the great ocean of the unknown stretches before us. Our eyes catch only the tiniest shimmer on the waves, yet even that glimpse fills us with awe. And Feynman would grin and add: isn't it marvelous that we can build instruments to peek at the rest?
